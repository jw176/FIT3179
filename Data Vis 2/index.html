Software Development in 2022

A visualisation by Jack Whelan
Data

The data presented in this visualisation is from the Stack Overflow Developer Survey 2022. The survey results and an analysis by Stack Overflow can be found here.

Demographics

The Stack Overflow Developer Survey saw over 70,000 developers participate from over 170 countries. This provides an excellent opportunity to explore and understand the complexities of the developer community.

If we examine the diversity of ethnicity, we can see that the most commonly reported ethnicity was "Multiracial" (which includes those that selected multiple options), with 20,036 developers, making up 28.84% of the respondents.

Taking a look at the range of genders amongst the developer community, we can see that men make up the overwhelming majority of developers, with 64,607 participants or 91.18% identifying themselves as a Man.

3,399 developers (4.80%) identified themselves as a Woman.

1,172 developers (1.65%) Preferred not to say.

And 704 developers (0.99%) identified themselves as Non-binary, genderqueer, or gender non-conforming.

Work Conditions

Examining the trends of remote work across the world in 2022, we can see there is quite a diverse range of in-person, hybrid, and remote developers.

Countries across North and South America mostly seem to have a majority of remote developers. Europe mostly consists of countries where the most common work type is a hybrid approach. Many countries across Asia tend to have mostly in-person developers. And in Africa, we see a variety of developer work types.

Perhaps it's the recent COVID pandemic, or the growing popularity of office stack tools like Slack and Microsoft Teams, or the fact that many developers only need a computer to do their work.

42.96% (25,203) of developers work Remotely.

42.51% (24,935) of developers have a Hyrbid workplace.

And 14.53% (8,525) of developers work strictly In-person.

Programming Languages

There are many features of a programming language that separate it from others. In order to analyse and compare different programming languages more reasonably, it helps to separate them into distinct groups that share some common features and characteristics.

Such mutually exclusive categorisations are hard to establish, however for the purpose of this visualisation, we will make the distinction between assembled languages (Assembly), languages that feature a major compilation step (Compiled Languages), languages that are interpreted at runtime (Interpreted Languages), and Markup Languages.
